[STARTS-WITH-ADVERB]
Imagine flipping open a tale and finding it begins with a crisp, telling adverb—"Suddenly," "Quietly," "Bravely." When a story opens that way, we mark it as [STARTS-WITH-ADVERB].

[TOPIC-CULTURAL-TRADITIONS]
When a story lives in the rituals, ceremonies, and passed-down practices of a people—when it revolves around festivals, rites, or the ways families keep their past alive—it wears the label [TOPIC-CULTURAL-TRADITIONS].

[THEME-INNOVATION]
If a narrative pulses with invention, new ideas, or the spirit of making things differently—that hunger to change the old into the new—then that story carries [THEME-INNOVATION].

[THEME-HUMOR]
If laughter, wit, or comic perspective threads through the plot and tone, the story is marked [THEME-HUMOR].

[TOPIC-HIDDEN-TREASURES]
When a tale turns on secret caches, forgotten riches, or buried things waiting to be found, it is about hidden troves and thus is [TOPIC-HIDDEN-TREASURES].

[STYLE-CLASSIC]
A story that reads like it belongs to a long lineage—timeless structure, traditional pacing, enduring diction—is given the stamp [STYLE-CLASSIC].

[STYLE-HUMOROUS]
When the overall voice and craft of the tale aim to amuse and delight, we call that [STYLE-HUMOROUS].

[STYLE-MODERN]
Stories that feel contemporary—fresh language, present concerns, a modern sensibility—are categorized as [STYLE-MODERN].

[TOPIC-GIANT-CREATURES]
If colossal beasts, towering animals, or creatures of outsized scale populate the narrative, the story falls under [TOPIC-GIANT-CREATURES].

[USES-PERFECT-ASPECT]
When the prose leans on constructions like "has done" or "had been," showing actions completed relative to other moments, the story [USES-PERFECT-ASPECT].

[HAS-A-FLASHBACK]
If the narrative turns back in time within the story—bringing a past scene to light as part of the present telling—it contains [HAS-A-FLASHBACK].

[THEME-HARDSHIP]
Stories that center on struggle, endurance, and the trials characters must face are suffused with [THEME-HARDSHIP].

[HAS-A-CLIFFHANGER]
When a tale leaves a vital question unresolved at the end, hanging the reader on a tense edge, that story has [HAS-A-CLIFFHANGER].

[HAS-A-MORAL-LESSON]
If a narrative clearly offers a lesson about right, wrong, or how to live, then it carries [HAS-A-MORAL-LESSON].

[TOPIC-LOST-CIVILIZATIONS]
Tales that explore abandoned cities, archaeological mysteries, and civilizations gone from history are labeled [TOPIC-LOST-CIVILIZATIONS].

[STYLE-PLAYFUL]
When the writing dances—full of light-hearted tricks, mischievous phrasing, and playful turns—it is [STYLE-PLAYFUL].

[TOPIC-SEASONAL-CHANGES]
Stories that revolve around the turning of seasons, harvests, and the cycle of nature belong to [TOPIC-SEASONAL-CHANGES].

[STARTS-WITH-PREPOSITION]
If the opening word is a preposition—"In," "On," "Under"—then the story is [STARTS-WITH-PREPOSITION].

[HAS-JUXTAPOSITION]
When a story places contrasting images, ideas, or scenes side by side to create fresh meaning, it displays [HAS-JUXTAPOSITION].

[THEME-PROBLEM-SOLVING]
If the heartbeat of the tale is puzzle, strategy, or characters overcoming obstacles through cleverness, it embodies [THEME-PROBLEM-SOLVING].

[TOPIC-TIME-TRAVEL]
Stories that carry characters between eras, sending them forward or backward through time, are marked [TOPIC-TIME-TRAVEL].

[TOPIC-BYGONE-ERAS]
When a narrative dwells in days long past—recreating vanished worlds and manners—it belongs to [TOPIC-BYGONE-ERAS].

[HAS-CIRCULAR-NARRATIVE-STRUCT]
If the plot loops back to its beginning or repeats structure in a ring, the story has [HAS-CIRCULAR-NARRATIVE-STRUCT].

[USES-DISCOURSE-MARKERS]
When a tale uses connectors like "however," "therefore," or "meanwhile" to guide its reasoning and flow, it [USES-DISCOURSE-MARKERS].

[USES-WH-QUESTIONS]
If the narrative voice asks who, what, where, when, why, or how within the text, it [USES-WH-QUESTIONS].

[TOPIC-THE-ARTS]
Stories that center on creation—painting, music, theater, dance, and the lives of artists—are classified as [TOPIC-THE-ARTS].

[THEME-MAGIC]
When wonder and supernatural forces shape the plot, letting the impossible become possible, the story is suffused with [THEME-MAGIC].

[STYLE-PHILOSOPHICAL]
If a narrative reflects on meaning, being, and big questions in a contemplative way, it is [STYLE-PHILOSOPHICAL].

[STYLE-MINIMALIST]
When the prose favors restraint—few words, tight scenes, open spaces—it qualifies as [STYLE-MINIMALIST].

[HAS-A-STORY-WITHIN-A-STORY]
If one narrative nests inside another, a tale telling another tale, then it contains [HAS-A-STORY-WITHIN-A-STORY].

[LONG-STORY]
A story that stretches beyond 375 words earns the tag [LONG-STORY].

[TOPIC-MYSTICAL-CREATURES]
If enchanted beings—fae, spirits, or otherworldly animals—play central roles, the story is [TOPIC-MYSTICAL-CREATURES].

[LOW-READABILITY]
When a text scores below 86.4 on readability measures and thus is harder to parse, we mark it [LOW-READABILITY].

[HAS-CHECKHOVS-GUN]
If an apparently minor detail is introduced and later becomes crucial—a classic Chekhov's gun—the story has [HAS-CHECKHOVS-GUN].

[MANY-PARAGRAPHS]
When a tale is organized into more than six paragraphs, it falls under [MANY-PARAGRAPHS].

[THEME-DECEPTION]
Stories that weave lies, disguises, and misleading appearances carry the theme [THEME-DECEPTION].

[THEME-AGENCY]
If characters take decisive action and drive the plot through their choices, the story exhibits [THEME-AGENCY].

[TOPIC-MAGICAL-LANDS]
When the setting itself is a wondrous realm—forests of enchantment, kingdoms beyond maps—the tale is about [TOPIC-MAGICAL-LANDS].

[USES-SUPERLATIVE-FORMS]
If the language stretches to extremes—"best," "most," "least"—the story [USES-SUPERLATIVE-FORMS].

[STARTS-WITH-ADJECTIVE]
When the first word that greets a reader is an adjective—"Ancient," "Lonely," "Bright"—the story is [STARTS-WITH-ADJECTIVE].

[THEME-KINDNESS]
If generosity, compassion, and caring steer the story’s moral compass, it carries [THEME-KINDNESS].

[HIGH-READABILITY]
When the prose flows effortlessly and scores above 96.1 on readability metrics, we call it [HIGH-READABILITY].

[STARTS-WITH-NOUN]
If the opening word names a person, place, or thing—"River," "Captain," "City"—the story is [STARTS-WITH-NOUN].

[USES-CONDITIONAL-MOOD]
When the narrative frames possibilities with "would," "could," or "if... then," it [USES-CONDITIONAL-MOOD].

[HAS-A-RED-HERRING]
If the plot deliberately misleads the reader with a distracting clue, that device is [HAS-A-RED-HERRING].

[SHORT-STORY]
A tale shorter than 160 words belongs to [SHORT-STORY].

[STYLE-SURREAL]
When a story bends reality into dreamlike, illogical, or bizarre shapes, it is [STYLE-SURREAL].

[THEME-HELPING-OTHERS]
If acts of assistance, solidarity, and altruism form the story’s heart, it has [THEME-HELPING-OTHERS].

[STYLE-LIGHTHEARTED]
When the tone is breezy, cheerful, and undemanding, the story reads as [STYLE-LIGHTHEARTED].
