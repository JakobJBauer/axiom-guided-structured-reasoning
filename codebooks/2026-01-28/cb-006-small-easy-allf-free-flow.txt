[HAS-A-FLASHBACK]
A story is [HAS-A-FLASHBACK] if The story has a flashback. In plain terms: tag a story with [HAS-A-FLASHBACK] any time it literally contains a flashback sequence.

[USES-WH-QUESTIONS]
A story is [USES-WH-QUESTIONS] if The story uses wh-questions. That means the narrative actually employs who/what/where/when/why/how questions.

[USES-DISCOURSE-MARKERS]
A story is [USES-DISCOURSE-MARKERS] if The story uses discourse markers. Use this label when the text relies on connectors and signposting (words or phrases that guide the reader through the discourse).

[INTERACTIVE]
A story is [INTERACTIVE] if exactly one of the following is true:
- The story is [USES-WH-QUESTIONS]
- The story is [USES-DISCOURSE-MARKERS]
So mark [INTERACTIVE] only when the story meets exactly one of those two conditions — not both, and not neither.

[COMPLEX-NARRATIVE]
A story is [COMPLEX-NARRATIVE] if both of the following are true:
- The story is [HAS-A-FLASHBACK]
- The story is not [INTERACTIVE]
That is, a complex narrative has a flashback and, at the same time, does not qualify as interactive under the rule above.

[SPECIAL-CASE]
A story is [SPECIAL-CASE] if either of the following is true:
- The story is in the set { [INTERACTIVE], [COMPLEX-NARRATIVE] }
- The story is equal to [HAS-A-FLASHBACK]
In other words, a special-case story is one that’s either interactive or a complex narrative, or it simply equals the [HAS-A-FLASHBACK] category.
